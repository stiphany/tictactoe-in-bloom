how to read the flower

The center is S₀, the empty grid. Nine stems leave it (X’s first move), each child has eight replies, then seven, and so on, but branches die early wherever someone completes three in a row, and many branches merge: X a1 then X b2 lands on the same position whether O answered in between at b1 or c3. Those merges are the lilac strands, the “two ways to get to the same state” from your notes.

Each ring holds every position reachable in the same number of moves, its blossoms spaced evenly around the circle like a row of dahlia petals, with alternate rows offset half a step. Ring spacing shrinks as the game goes on, so a move early in the game is a long stem and a move late in the game is a short petal. That makes speed visible: the fastest wins (X in 5 moves) sit close to the heart of the flower at the end of long, sparse stems, crowned with little gold rays, while long grinding games gather as fine lace on the rim.

Folding the 8 symmetries (4 rotations × mirrors) treats boards that are the same flower viewed from a different angle as one blossom: 5,478 positions collapse to 765, and the count of complete games, weighted by how many raw boards each blossom stands for, still totals 255,168.
